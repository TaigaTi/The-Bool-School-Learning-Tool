Simplifying Boolean Expressions Using Karnaugh Maps

Now that we understand the history of Karnaugh Maps as well as the various types, we can now dive in to understanding how to simplify them. This simplification is done through the grouping of minterms.

Grouping Minterms

In a 3-input Karnaugh map, any two horizontally or vertically adjacent minterms, each made up of three variables, can be joined to create a new product term made up of just two variables.Likewise, any two horizontally or vertically adjacent minterms in a four-input map can be joined to create a new product term with just three variables.

The 1s connected to the minterms can also be used to create various groupings. In fact, any group of 2^n adjacent minterms can be gathered together (where n is a positive integer). For example, 22 = two minterms, 22 = 2 × 2 = four minterms, 23 = 2 × 2 × 2 = eight minterms, and so forth.

Groupings may also wrap from adjacent edges of the Karnaugh map.

Since the top-bottom rows and the left-right columns are logically adjacent, the four corner minterms are likewise logically adjacent, which in turn means they may be used to make a single group even though diagonally adjacent minterms typically cannot be used to form a group.

Populating Maps Using 0s VS 1s

In the case of a function whose output is logic 1 less frequently than it is logic 0, it is typically simpler to extract an equation in the sum-of-products form when extracting Boolean equations from truth tables. Likewise, it is typically simpler to extract an equation in the product-of-sums form if the result is logic 0 less frequently than it is logic 1. These two forms of expressions will produce identical results.

Three- and four-input functions are most frequently represented as Karnaugh maps. Similar maps can be made for functions with 5 and 6 inputs, but they can easily become cumbersome and challenging to use. As a result, it is generally accepted that the Karnaugh approach is not suitable for functions that have more than six inputs.
