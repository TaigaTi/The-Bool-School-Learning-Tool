History of the Maps

The History of the Maps Stand on 3 Pillars:

The Tree of Porphyry

From as early as 384 BC Diagrams used to represent logical concepts have been present in one form or another. One Example of this is, in 384-322 BC Aristotle was very familiar with the idea of using a stylized tree figure to represent the relationships between things such as different species. Diagrams of this type, AKA the Tree of Porphyry, are often found in medieval pictures.

John Venn

The English logician John Venn (1834-1923) proposed a graphical method to display logical relationships between groups of "things" in a paper titled "On the Diagrammatic and Mechanical Representation of Propositions and Reasonings" that was published in the Philosophical Magazine and Journal of Science in 1890. The work of English mathematician George Boole had a significant impact on Venn, and his Venn diagrams greatly enhanced Boolean algebra.

Maurice Karnaugh

Venn diagrams were heavily based on how overlapping circles or ellipses interacted with one another. Later works by authors like the Reverend Charles Lutwidge Dodgson (1832-1898) and Allan Marquand (1853-1924), a lecturer in logic and ethics at John Hopkins University, were based on squares and rectangles. A Karnaugh map, created in 1953 by American physicist Maurice Karnaugh (pronounced "car-no,"), offers an alternate method for encoding Boolean functions. For instance, look at the Karnaugh map to your right for a 2-input AND function.
